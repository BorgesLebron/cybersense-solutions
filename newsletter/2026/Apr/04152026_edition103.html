Daily Digital Awareness Brief

The Trusted Tool Is the Attack

CPUID's website was compromised for less than 24 hours – long enough to serve trojanized versions of CPU-Z and HWMonitor to every user who downloaded during that window. A malicious IDE extension masquerading as a WakaTime productivity tracker deployed the GlassWorm dropper across developer machines. Storm-2755 is rerouting Canadian employee payroll deposits by hijacking accounts through credential theft. APT28 is using steganography and legitimate cloud services to hide command-and-control traffic for a new malware suite targeting Ukraine and NATO allies.

The pattern in all four incidents is the same: the attack arrived through something the victim trusted. A hardware utility downloaded from its official vendor. A productivity extension in a legitimate marketplace. A payroll system login. A cloud service used for legitimate file sharing. When attackers operate through trusted channels, the traditional question – "did this come from a known-bad source?" – stops being useful. The better question is: "does this behavior make sense for what this tool should be doing?" That behavioral lens is what this edition is built around.

Situational Awareness

CPUID Website Compromised – CPU-Z and HWMonitor Downloads Weaponized for 19 Hours

Threat actors compromised CPUID's official website and replaced legitimate downloads of CPU-Z, HWMonitor, HWMonitor Pro, and PerfMonitor with trojanized executables carrying a remote access trojan called STX RAT. The compromise window ran from approximately April 9 at 15:00 UTC to April 10 at 10:00 UTC – roughly 19 hours. Any user who downloaded any of these tools during that window should assume the installed software is malicious and treat the affected system as compromised. CPU-Z and HWMonitor are among the most widely used hardware diagnostic tools among IT professionals, system administrators, and technical users – making CPUID a high-value target for a supply chain attack. This incident follows the same pattern as the Smart Slider 3 Pro compromise covered in Edition 101: trusted update and download channels subverted to deliver weaponized builds to users who are following best practice by downloading from official sources.

APT28 Deploys PRISMEX Malware Against Ukraine and NATO Allies Using Steganography and Cloud Abuse

The Russian state-sponsored threat actor APT28 – also tracked as Forest Blizzard and Pawn Storm – has been linked to a new spear-phishing campaign deploying a previously undocumented malware suite called PRISMEX against targets in Ukraine and NATO-aligned countries. PRISMEX combines advanced steganography to hide payloads inside image files, COM hijacking for privilege escalation, and abuse of legitimate cloud services for command-and-control communication – making the traffic blend with normal organizational network activity. For security teams monitoring network traffic, the use of legitimate cloud services as C2 channels is a significant detection challenge: blocking the services is operationally disruptive, but allowing unrestricted traffic to them provides adversaries with a reliable, low-visibility communication channel. Behavioral monitoring of what processes are communicating with cloud services – and whether that communication pattern is consistent with the process's expected function – is the more viable detection approach.

Storm-2755 Intercepting Canadian Payroll Deposits Through Employee Account Hijacking

A financially motivated threat actor tracked as Storm-2755 has been observed compromising Canadian employee accounts and redirecting salary deposits to attacker-controlled accounts in what security researchers are calling payroll pirate attacks. The attack chain begins with credential theft – typically through phishing or infostealer malware – followed by account takeover and modification of direct deposit banking information before the next payroll cycle processes. The incident is a useful reminder that financial fraud through account compromise does not require breaching the employer's payroll system directly. Compromising a single employee account with the right permissions is sufficient. Organizations with self-service HR portals that allow employees to update banking information should ensure those changes require secondary verification – a confirmation email to the address on file, a time delay before changes take effect, or manager approval for banking updates are all effective controls against this specific attack pattern.

Training Byte

How to Audit Your Browser Extensions – Ten Minutes That Close a Real Gap

Why it matters:

Browser extensions have direct access to everything you see and type in the browser – including credentials, session tokens, internal systems, and sensitive documents. Most people install them once and never review them again. The GlassWorm campaign exploited exactly this behavior, hiding a malicious dropper inside an extension that looked like a legitimate developer tool.

The ten-minute audit:

In Chrome: Settings → Extensions → Review each one. For every extension, ask: do I still use this? Do I remember installing it? Does its permission list match what it should need? An extension that reads all your browsing data, accesses cookies, and can run remote scripts – but whose stated purpose is a simple task like tab management – is suspicious. Remove anything you don't actively use, don't recognize, or that has permissions inconsistent with its stated function. Do this quarterly. It takes ten minutes and removes an entire category of risk that most security tools don't monitor.

The extension you forgot you installed is the one attackers are counting on.

Modernization and AI Insight

GlassWorm Evolves – Zig Dropper Now Infects All IDEs on a Developer's Machine Through a Malicious Extension

The GlassWorm campaign has evolved to use a new Zig-language dropper delivered through a malicious Open VSX extension named "specstudio.code-wakatime-activity-tracker," which impersonates the legitimate WakaTime developer productivity tracker. Once installed, the dropper identifies and infects all integrated development environments on the machine – not just the one where the extension was installed. For organizations where developers use AI coding assistants, IDE extensions, and productivity tools as a routine part of their workflow, this campaign highlights a specific risk: the developer toolchain has become an attractive attack surface precisely because developer machines typically have broad access to source code, internal systems, cloud credentials, and deployment pipelines. Extension vetting – checking publisher identity, install count, and permission scope before installation – should be part of the standard developer onboarding security checklist.

Critical Alert Volume Up 400% – AI-Assisted Development Is Outpacing Remediation Workflows

OX Security analyzed 216 million security findings across 250 organizations over a 90-day period and found that while raw alert volume grew 52 percent year-over-year, prioritized critical risk grew by nearly 400 percent. The ratio of critical findings to raw alerts nearly tripled – from 0.035 percent to 0.092 percent. The driver is the acceleration of AI-assisted development: code is being written and deployed faster than security review processes can keep pace with, increasing the density of high-impact vulnerabilities in production environments. For security teams evaluating their current tooling, this data makes the case for shifting from volume-based alert triage to contextual risk prioritization – focusing remediation resources on the subset of findings that represent actual exploitable risk in production, rather than attempting to work through the full alert queue. The velocity gap is a structural problem that more tooling alone won't solve; it requires process changes in how development and security teams collaborate at the speed of AI-assisted deployment.

Final Thought

Every incident in this edition has the same root: something trusted was the vector. A legitimate download site. A known extension marketplace. A payroll portal. A cloud storage service. The practical implication is not that you should stop trusting tools – that's operationally impossible. It's that trust should be time-limited and behavior-verified. The tool you installed and reviewed six months ago may not be the same tool running on your machine today. The extension that had reasonable permissions when you installed it may have quietly expanded them since. Periodic review of what you've trusted, and whether its behavior still matches what you expect, is not paranoia. It is the discipline that the current threat landscape requires.
